FOUNDATIONAL ESSAY

THORACIC OUTLET SYNDROME DIAGNOSTIC DELAY

Six Years of Documented Vascular Compression: 2019-2025

Compiled: 18 January 2026

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This essay documents a SIX-YEAR diagnostic delay for Thoracic Outlet Syndrome (TOS), with objective imaging evidence of vascular compression dating to 2019 that was repeatedly dismissed or lost. The 2019 Doppler ultrasound showed "complete cessation of flow" during provocative manoeuvres—a POSITIVE result that was not acted upon. In June 2025, repeat testing confirmed bilateral arterial AND venous TOS with complete vascular occlusion on arm abduction. This delay represents a separate diagnostic failure from the fracture delay, with its own causation and quantum implications.

PART ONE: THE 2019 POSITIVE DOPPLER (F032)

1.1 The Royal Free Doppler Study

On 13 June 2019, a bilateral upper-limb arterial duplex study (TOS protocol) was performed at the Royal Free Hospital by Lily Bishop, Clinical Vascular Scientist. The results were unequivocal:

"Bilaterally, during the Adson's manoeuvre and the EAST manoeuvre, and also on the left side during the Military manoeuvre, there was complete cessation of flow within the distal subclavian artery. Patient experienced pain, coolness and tingling in these positions. These results were repeatable."

CONCLUSION: "Marked haemodynamic changes upon the majority of tested TOS positions - a positive result."

Despite this unequivocal positive result, the Doppler report was lost or not acted upon. The GP practice did not follow up on the TOS diagnosis. For FIVE YEARS, from 2019 to 2024, John experienced progressive upper limb symptoms without appropriate management.

PART FOUR: STATIC vs DYNAMIC IMAGING

4.1 The Critical Distinction

TOS is a DYNAMIC condition. Vascular compression occurs when the arm is in specific positions (abduction, elevation). Static imaging with arms at sides will appear NORMAL even in severe TOS. This has critical implications:

• CT/MRI with arms down CANNOT exclude TOS

• Only dynamic imaging (Doppler with provocative manoeuvres, or MR with arms abducted) can diagnose

• Normal static imaging does NOT rebut positive dynamic findings

4.2 April 2024 CT Report (F037)

The UCLH CT Aorta (16/04/2024) was performed with arms DOWN. The report explicitly stated: "A thoracic outlet syndrome affecting left subclavian artery cannot be excluded" and recommended duplex ultrasound with arms up/down for further assessment.

This demonstrates proper clinical understanding: static imaging CANNOT rule out TOS. Any clinician dismissing TOS based on arms-down imaging demonstrates a failure to understand the condition.

5.2 Breach Analysis

• DUTY: GP practice received 2019 Doppler results showing positive TOS.

• STANDARD: Positive TOS on Doppler requires vascular surgery follow-up and management.

• BREACH: Results lost or not actioned for 5+ years.

• DAMAGE: Progressive vascular symptoms, now bilateral arterial AND venous TOS confirmed.

CONCLUSIONS

1. OBJECTIVE EVIDENCE FROM 2019: Complete arterial flow cessation on provocative testing represents unequivocal positive TOS diagnosis (F032).

2. SIX-YEAR DELAY: From 2019 positive test to 2025 diagnosis represents a separate diagnostic failure pathway with its own causation and quantum.

3. PROGRESSIVE WORSENING: 2025 testing now shows bilateral arterial AND venous TOS—worse than 2019 when only arterial compression was documented.

4. STATIC IMAGING IRRELEVANT: Any dismissal based on arms-down CT/MRI demonstrates fundamental misunderstanding of TOS pathophysiology.

5. PATIENT HAD TO PAY PRIVATELY: Initial TOS diagnosis (2019) only occurred because patient privately consulted Dr Brookes. GP had refused vascular referral despite visible pathology.
