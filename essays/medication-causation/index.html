Executive Summary

This essay documents the direct causation chain linking medication mismanagement by the GP practice to the December 2021 tonic-clonic seizure that caused T5-T8 vertebral fractures. The chain is not speculative—it is explicitly documented by treating specialists who wrote: “When had a seizure pregabalin had been stopped abruptly” (F324). This constitutes the clearest possible causation evidence in a clinical negligence case.

Part One: The BNF Breach — Pregabalin Cessation

1.1 The Medication Protocol Violation

In October 2021, John Thomson was prescribed Pregabalin 600mg daily for chronic pain management. The British National Formulary (BNF) mandates gradual tapering when discontinuing pregabalin due to established seizure risk. What occurred violated this fundamental prescribing guidance:

1.2 The BNF Requirement

The British National Formulary explicitly states that pregabalin should be tapered gradually over at least one week to avoid withdrawal effects including SEIZURES. An 83% dose reduction over days, followed by months of supply void, constitutes a clear breach of prescribing guidance.

Part Three: Repeated Pattern — GP Medication Interference

The pregabalin cessation was not an isolated incident. A documented pattern of GP interference with specialist-prescribed medications emerged:

3.1 Baclofen Stopped Despite 90% Efficacy (F325, F196)

Dr Simeoni documented that Baclofen achieved “90% resolution” of tremor (F325). This response to a GABAergic medication directly challenges any functional tremor diagnosis—functional tremor would NOT respond to baclofen. Despite this documented efficacy, the GP subsequently stopped the medication (F196).

3.2 OxyContin Stopped Unsafely — Seizure Risk Documented (F334)

“Abruptly stopping oxycontin is unsafe as the patient is at risk of seizures and is at high risk of withdrawal”

— Pain Clinic Nurse, Chelsea & Westminster, 23 November 2023 (CL106)

In a patient with KNOWN EPILEPSY, the GP took action that the Pain Clinic explicitly documented as unsafe and creating seizure risk. This is not a matter of clinical judgment—this is documented breach of standard of care.

3.3 Lamotrigine Removal Crisis (F102)

Following practice transfer, anti-epileptic Lamotrigine was “deducted” from the record. When patient requested reinstatement, Dr Arunachalam refused, documenting she “could not see why it was started”—despite the patient having documented tonic-clonic seizure history and “Generalised Convulsive Epilepsy” on the active problem list. This represents fundamental incompetence: failing to link a seizure diagnosis with standard anti-epileptic medication.

4.2 Formal Epilepsy Diagnosis Confirms Validity

In December 2024, Dr Karunaratne formally diagnosed “Epilepsy with generalised seizures” (F354). This validates the causation chain: the December 2021 seizure was a genuine epileptic event (not dissociative/functional), causally linked to abrupt pregabalin cessation, now controlled with appropriate anti-epileptic medication (lamotrigine).

Conclusions

  1. BREACH ESTABLISHED: The 83% pregabalin reduction without proper tapering violated BNF guidance. No responsible body of medical opinion would support this prescribing decision.

  2. CAUSATION DOCUMENTED: Dr Simeoni’s contemporaneous statement (F324) provides direct specialist documentation of the causal link between medication cessation and seizure.

  3. PATTERN ESTABLISHED: Repeated instances of GP medication interference (F325, F334, F102) demonstrate systemic prescribing failures, not isolated error.

  4. EPILEPSY VALIDATED: Formal diagnosis (F354) confirms the seizures were genuine epileptic events, now controlled with appropriate medication—proving the condition was always treatable had it been properly managed.
