FOUNDATIONAL ESSAY

PRACTICE MANAGER CONDUCT

The Non-Clinical Actor Making Clinical Decisions: Clive Polles

Compiled: 18 January 2026

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This essay documents the conduct of Clive Polles, Practice Manager at Grove Park Terrace Surgery/Glebe Street Surgery. Polles is a non-clinical administrative staff member who made clinical decisions about patient medication—most critically, refusing to authorise a replacement buprenorphine patch 4 days before the seizure that caused vertebral fractures. His conduct extends to off-record mobile phone contact with the patient's family, false claims about medication quantities, and behaviour that Dr Gorrie (current GP) referenced as "sketchy stuff" that blocked a practice merger.

PART ONE: THE BUPRENORPHINE PATCH INCIDENT

1.1 The Critical Event (F072)

In mid-December 2021, approximately 4 days before the index seizure, John Thomson's buprenorphine transdermal patch became water-damaged in the bath. This was a fresh patch, clearly recently applied, that required replacement. The surgery was closing for Christmas.

Clive Polles—the Practice Manager, NOT a GP or clinician—refused to authorise the replacement patch.

The patient was left without adequate pain control over the Christmas period. Four days later, on 23 December 2021, he suffered a tonic-clonic seizure that caused T5-T8 vertebral fractures.

1.2 Why This Matters

ISSUE

SIGNIFICANCE

Non-clinical actor

Practice managers have NO authority over clinical decisions. Medication authorisation is a clinical function requiring clinical judgment.

Timing

4 days before seizure. Patient left without pain medication immediately preceding the index injury.

Causation link

Inadequate pain control → sleep disruption → lowered seizure threshold (documented risk factor) → seizure → fractures

GP oversight failure

The practice allowed non-clinical staff to make clinical decisions without proper supervision or escalation.

PART TWO: OFF-THE-BOOKS CONTACT (F070, F071)

2.1 Personal Mobile Phone Contact

Clive Polles made telephone calls to the patient's mother from his PERSONAL MOBILE PHONE (not the practice line) regarding clinical matters including medication. These calls left no official record trail.

This raises critical questions:

• Why contact a patient's family from a personal phone about clinical matters?

• Why avoid the audit trail that practice phone systems provide?

• Why would a practice manager need to discuss medication quantities with family members?

2.2 False Claims About Medication

During these off-record calls, Polles made claims to John's mother about the quantities of medication John was receiving. These claims were FALSE and immediately disprovable because:

John's father collected every single prescription. Complete records available.

The practice knew exactly what medication John was receiving. The false claims to his mother cannot be explained by error—the records were available to verify.

2.3 The "Drug Mule" Accusation

Polles asked John whether he was "being made to give the medication to his mother." This accusation—that John was diverting controlled substances to a family member—was made by a non-clinical administrator, via personal mobile phone, without evidence.

iMessage evidence (F136) documents family discussion of this incident: "Clive's insane comments about making you my drug mule" (08/07/2022).

3.3 Pre-Seizure Pattern

Messages from July-November 2021—ALL PREDATING the 23/12/21 seizure—document medication withdrawal concerns, formal complaint contemplation, and referral tracking failures. This establishes the pattern was recognised contemporaneously, not retrospectively invented for litigation.

PART FOUR: THIRD-PARTY CORROBORATION

4.1 Dr Gorrie's "Sketchy Stuff" Reference (F021)

Dr Alexander Gorrie, John's current GP at a different practice, made verbal reference to "sketchy stuff" that Clive Polles had done, in the context of explaining why a proposed practice merger was blocked.

This is significant because: it comes from an independent NHS clinician with no connection to the litigation; it references conduct serious enough to block an organisational merger; and it corroborates the pattern documented in patient testimony and iMessages.

4.2 Dr Karunaratne's "Data Manipulation" Statement (F020)

Dr Kushan Karunaratne, Consultant Neurologist at Imperial College Healthcare NHS Trust, spontaneously stated "data manipulation" regarding the GP records during a clinic appointment. John's parents were present as witnesses. This was not a patient allegation—it was an independent specialist observation.

PART FIVE: LEGAL SIGNIFICANCE

5.1 How This Strengthens the Negligence Claim

1. Explains why the record/care pathway became distorted—non-clinical actor making clinical decisions.

2. Opens separate legal liabilities: confidentiality breach, data protection violation, potentially malicious falsehood.

3. Supports limitation argument if deliberate concealment can be established.

5.2 The Systemic Breach Framing

If a practice manager was the effective decision-maker blocking clinically indicated prescribing, this can be framed as: systemic breach by the practice for allowing non-clinical staff to control clinical decisions; AND/OR breach by GPs who allowed non-clinical staff to control access without oversight.

CONCLUSIONS

1. NON-CLINICAL DECISION-MAKING: Clive Polles made clinical decisions about medication without clinical authority. The buprenorphine patch refusal 4 days before seizure is the critical event.

2. AUDIT TRAIL AVOIDANCE: Personal mobile phone contact about clinical matters leaves no record. This is incompatible with proper clinical governance.

3. FALSE CLAIMS: Statements about medication quantities were demonstrably false given complete prescription collection records.

4. THIRD-PARTY CORROBORATION: Dr Gorrie ("sketchy stuff") and Dr Karunaratne ("data manipulation") independently reference conduct problems, corroborating patient account.

5. CONTEMPORANEOUS DOCUMENTATION: iMessage evidence (F136) provides real-time family documentation spanning 2021-2026, establishing awareness predating litigation.
