THE DIAGNOSTIC DELAY

Eleven Months from Seizure to Fracture Diagnosis

THOMSON v NHS PROVIDERS

Clinical Negligence Case Reference

Compiled: 17 January 2026

Document Status: FOUNDATIONAL EVIDENTIAL COMPILATION

SUBJECT TO LEGAL PROFESSIONAL PRIVILEGE

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This document establishes the factual foundation for a clinical negligence claim arising from an eleven-month delay in diagnosing T5-T8 vertebral fractures sustained during a tonic-clonic seizure on 23 December 2021. The fractures were not confirmed until MRI on 16 October 2022, despite the patient presenting with persistent thoracic pain throughout this period.

The delay resulted from a systematic pattern of diagnostic overshadowing, where objective physical pathology was repeatedly attributed to psychiatric causes without adequate investigation. This pattern is evidenced by contemporaneous documentation from multiple independent sources, including ambulance records, specialist letters, and imaging reports.

KEY FACTS AT A GLANCE

Index Event: 23 December 2021 - Witnessed tonic-clonic seizure (Ambulance ePCR F250)

Diagnosis Date: 16 October 2022 - MRI confirms T5-T8 vertebral fractures

Diagnostic Delay: Approximately 11 months

Evidence Status: VERIFIED against Tier 1-3 contemporaneous documentation

Breach Indicator: YES - Standard of care required spinal imaging following high-energy trauma

PART ONE: THE INDEX EVENT

1.1 The First Seizure: 23 December 2021

On the evening of 23 December 2021, John Thomson experienced his first witnessed tonic-clonic seizure at his home in Chiswick. This event is documented by the London Ambulance Service ePCR (Electronic Patient Care Record), designated F250 in the evidence index.

Ambulance Response Documentation

The London Ambulance Service received the emergency call at 22:39 hours. The ambulance arrived on scene at 22:44 hours, a response time of five minutes. This rapid response is significant: under NHS Ambulance Response Programme standards, a five-minute response indicates the call was triaged as Category 1 (Life Threatening) or high Category 2 (Emergency).

Finding Reference: F023a - The five-minute response time objectively demonstrates that the ambulance service triaged this as a serious emergency. This categorisation directly contradicts any later suggestion that the December 2021 event was a low-acuity episode.

1.2 The Mechanism of Injury

A tonic-clonic seizure involves violent muscular contractions affecting the entire body. During the tonic phase, sustained muscle contraction can generate forces sufficient to cause vertebral compression fractures, particularly in patients with reduced bone mineral density. The clonic phase involves rhythmic jerking movements that compound this trauma.

The ambulance documentation that the patient was "extremely combative" and "held to ground by father" indicates the severity of the muscular activity during this event. The subsequent administration of Entonox for back pain demonstrates immediate spinal symptoms.

Causation Chain Established: Tonic-clonic seizure (23 December 2021) → Violent muscular contractions → T5-T8 vertebral fractures → Persistent thoracic pain → 11-month diagnostic delay

PART TWO: THE DIAGNOSTIC DELAY

2.1 Initial GP Response: "Soft Tissue Injury"

On 31 December 2021, eight days after the seizure, John Thomson attended his GP practice. The GP record (Entry 2021-350) documents the visit but characterises the injury as "soft tissue." This characterisation is significant for two reasons:

It demonstrates that the patient sought medical attention for back pain within days of the seizure, establishing continuity of symptoms;

It reveals that the GP did not consider the possibility of spinal fracture following a witnessed seizure, despite this being a well-recognised complication.

Finding Reference: F001 - The "soft tissue" characterisation contradicts the eventual MRI findings of vertebral fractures. This entry demonstrates the initial diagnostic failure that set the pattern for the subsequent delay.

2.2 The Pattern of Dismissal: Pre-Seizure Context

The diagnostic delay must be understood in the context of pre-existing GP attitudes towards this patient. Critical evidence establishes that the pattern of dismissal preceded the seizure and fractures.

MSK Physiotherapy Documentation: August 2021

On 4 August 2021, approximately four months before the seizure, Sumeer Bhamm (Senior MSK Physiotherapist, Charing Cross Hospital) documented the following during a face-to-face assessment:

"He has had issues with the GP in the past, as they did not believe his pain and accused him for attention seeking."

— Sumeer Bhamm, Senior MSK Physiotherapist, 4 August 2021 (Entry 2021-269)

Finding Reference: F022 - This contemporaneous third-party documentation of GP dismissal PREDATES the seizure and fractures by four months. It establishes that the dismissive attitude was not a response to the patient's presentation after the injury, but a pre-existing pattern that contributed to the diagnostic delay.

The Significance of Face-to-Face Assessment

Sumeer Bhamm's assessment is particularly significant because it involved direct physical examination. The letter documents: "He ensures [assures] me that he is not suicidal but just struggling to manage his pain." This demonstrates that an independent clinician who physically examined the patient found his presentation credible.

Finding Reference: F027 - Bhamm's November 2021 letter (23 November 2021) documents that the Pain Clinic had rejected the patient's referral because "his mother had spoken to your department and mentioned that the patient's low mood may make him suicidal." The patient was never spoken to directly before discharge. This occurred 30 days before the seizure.

2.3 The Neurospine Dismissal: February 2022

In February 2022, approximately two months after the seizure, the patient was seen by the Neurospine service. Despite ongoing thoracic symptoms, the service discharged the patient without identifying the fractures.

"MRI doesn't explain pain"

— Neurospine Service, February 2022 - Patient discharged (F347)

Finding Reference: F347 - The Neurospine service imaged the wrong spinal region. The patient's symptoms related to the thoracic spine (T5-T8), but imaging focused on a different area. This represents a failure to adequately investigate the mechanism of injury. The patient was discharged eight months before the fractures were eventually identified.

2.4 Somatoform Diagnosis: January 2023

On 5 January 2023, Dr Bulale Ali applied a Somatoform Pain Disorder diagnosis (Entry 2023-011). This diagnosis was applied during a telephone consultation about opioid medication switching, not during any psychiatric assessment. The patient was not informed that this diagnosis had been added to his records.

Critical Breach Indicators:

No psychiatric assessment conducted prior to diagnosis

Diagnosis applied during unrelated telephone consultation

Patient not informed diagnosis had been added

Wrong ICD-10 code used (E278 - endocrine disorder - instead of F45.4 - somatoform pain disorder)

Applied AFTER MRI had already confirmed T5-T8 fractures (October 2022)

Finding References: F023 (somatoform without assessment), F040 (applied during medication call), F041 (wrong ICD code)

PART THREE: THE DIAGNOSIS

3.1 MRI Confirmation: 16 October 2022

On 16 October 2022, an MRI scan finally confirmed T5-T8 vertebral fractures. This objective imaging evidence represents Tier 1 evidence in the evidence hierarchy: indisputable, objective findings that cannot be attributed to psychological factors.

MRI FINDINGS - 16 OCTOBER 2022

T5-T8 Vertebral Fractures Confirmed

Evidence Tier: 1 (Objective Imaging - Highest Reliability)

Diagnostic Delay: Approximately 11 months from index event (23 December 2021)

3.2 The Vindication: Dr Gorrie's Action

On 22 July 2025, Dr Alexander Gorrie, the patient's current GP, marked the Somatoform Pain Disorder diagnosis as INACTIVE, adding the clinical note: "there were clear causes for his pain."

"There were clear causes for his pain."

— Dr Alexander Gorrie, 22 July 2025 (F030)

Finding Reference: F030 - This represents internal NHS vindication. A subsequent GP independently determined that the psychiatric diagnosis was inappropriate and removed it without prompting from the patient. Dr Gorrie did not require a formal psychiatric reassessment to remove the diagnosis because the error was self-evident.

PART FOUR: BREACH ANALYSIS

4.1 Standard of Care: Bolam/Bolitho

Under the Bolam test, a medical practitioner is not negligent if they act in accordance with a practice accepted as proper by a responsible body of medical practitioners. Under Bolitho, that practice must withstand logical analysis.

The Standard for Post-Seizure Assessment

A responsible body of medical practitioners would recognise that:

Tonic-clonic seizures involve violent muscular contractions capable of causing vertebral fractures;

A patient presenting with back pain immediately following a witnessed seizure requires spinal imaging to exclude fracture;

The absence of neurological deficit does not exclude vertebral fracture;

Psychiatric diagnoses should not be applied without appropriate psychiatric assessment.

PART FIVE: CONCLUSIONS AND SIGNIFICANCE

This document establishes, through contemporaneous documentary evidence, that:

John Thomson sustained T5-T8 vertebral fractures during a witnessed tonic-clonic seizure on 23 December 2021;

These fractures were not diagnosed until MRI on 16 October 2022, a delay of approximately 11 months;

The delay resulted from a pre-existing pattern of dismissal documented by independent clinicians before the seizure occurred;

A somatoform diagnosis was inappropriately applied without psychiatric assessment, during an unrelated telephone call, using the wrong diagnostic code, without informing the patient;

Multiple independent specialists have refuted the psychiatric characterisation;

The patient's current GP independently removed the somatoform diagnosis, noting "there were clear causes for his pain."

The evidence compiled in this document is drawn from Tier 1-3 sources: objective imaging, independent specialist letters, and ambulance records. GP entries from the former practice are corroborated against these higher-tier sources where relied upon.
