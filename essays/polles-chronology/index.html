Practice Manager Conduct: Complete Chronology

Documented Timeline of Non-Clinical Interference in Clinical Care

Date: 2026-01-21
Status: CURRENT

Executive Summary

This essay presents a comprehensive chronological documentation of practice manager Clive Polles’ involvement in clinical decisions at Grove Park Terrace Surgery and Glebe Street Surgery. The evidence is drawn from contemporaneous family communications — primarily iMessage exchanges — that document a pattern of non-clinical interference spanning 2021-2026.

The documented pattern includes:

  • Direct medication decisions by a non-clinician
  • Appointment cancellations by the practice manager
  • Records access blocking attributed to Polles
  • Origination of the “drug abuse” narrative witnessed and documented
  • Buprenorphine refusal 4 days before the index seizure

Pattern Indicators

The contemporaneous communications reveal a consistent pattern:

  • Non-clinician making clinical decisions — medication authorisation, prescription timing
  • Originating false narratives — the “drug abuse” accusation traced to Clive
  • Blocking patient access — both to records and to appointments
  • Making off-record phone calls — to family members with disprovable claims
  • Pattern of false statements — documented lies about appointment rules
  • Managing multiple practices — influence extending across surgeries

Connection to Current GP’s Statement

Dr Gorrie (John’s current GP) referenced “sketchy stuff” that was blocking a practice merger involving the surgeries Clive managed. This independent observation from a clinician uninvolved in the original events corroborates the pattern of problematic conduct.

Legal Significance

Regulatory Gap

As family members noted: “Clive isn’t answerable to the GMC.” Practice managers are not clinicians and fall outside GMC jurisdiction. However, this does not immunise:

  • The practice from vicarious liability
  • Individual clinicians who acted on Clive’s direction
  • The practice from CQC oversight of governance

Potential Claims

  • Vicarious liability for practice manager’s conduct
  • Negligence in allowing non-clinician to make clinical decisions
  • Data protection breaches (records access blocking, false entries)
  • Contract breaches (failure to provide care)

Conclusion

The contemporaneous family communications paint a comprehensive picture of a practice manager who exceeded his authority in ways that directly impacted patient care. The pattern spans five years and includes:

  • Direct medication decisions documented in text messages
  • Origination of the drug abuse narrative (witnessed)
  • Blocking patient access to records and appointments
  • Making “off the books” calls with false claims

The evidence is not retrospective allegation — it is contemporaneous documentation from family members recording events as they occurred. The timestamps, the specificity, and the corroboration across multiple family members establish a pattern that goes far beyond administrative function into clinical interference.

The December 2025 note that Grove Park “dropped Clive like a hot brick” suggests the practice itself eventually recognised the problem — but only after years of harm to the patient.

This chronology is compiled from contemporaneous family communications extracted from iMessage database.
