## Executive Summary

John Thomson has **documented Thoracic Outlet Syndrome (TOS)** on imaging studies spanning **over six years** — from June 2019 to the present. Despite this objective evidence of vascular compression, the condition has not received definitive treatment.

The symptom severity has been documented by specialists: the patient has stated he would *"rather his arm be amputated"* than continue living with the unrelieved symptoms.

## Understanding Thoracic Outlet Syndrome

### What Is TOS?

Thoracic Outlet Syndrome refers to compression of nerves, arteries, or veins in the **thoracic outlet** — the space between the collarbone and first rib.

**Types:**
- **Neurogenic TOS** — Nerve compression (most common)
- **Venous TOS** — Vein compression
- **Arterial TOS** — Artery compression (rarest, most dangerous)

John Thomson has documented **vascular TOS** with evidence of both arterial and venous compression.

### Symptoms

TOS can cause:
- Pain in neck, shoulder, and arm
- Numbness and tingling
- Weakness in hand/arm
- Colour changes in hand
- Swelling
- Blood clots (in vascular forms)

## The Six-Year Timeline

### 2019: First Positive Study

**13 June 2019 — Duplex Ultrasound, Royal Free Hospital**

> *"Complete cessation of flow within the distal subclavian artery during Adson's and EAST manoeuvres bilaterally. Marked haemodynamic changes noted... This is a positive result."*

This is unambiguous. In 2019, objective testing demonstrated vascular compression causing complete flow cessation.

### 2024: MR Confirmation

**10 January 2024 — MR Thoracic Inlet, UCLH**

Dr An Thanh Ngo reported:
> *"Compression of the left subclavian artery and vein in the subclavicular space with arms abducted"*

And:
> *"Apparent focal stenosis"*

Five years after the first positive study, imaging confirms ongoing vascular compression.

### 2024: CT Cannot Exclude TOS

**16 April 2024 — CT Aorta, UCLH**

The radiologist specifically noted:
> *"A thoracic outlet syndrome affecting left subclavian artery cannot be excluded on static imaging"*

The radiologist acknowledged the limitations of the study and recommended dynamic assessment.

### 2025: Doppler Confirms Bilateral Occlusion

**9 June 2025 — Duplex Doppler, Royal Free Hospital**

> *"At 90° abduction, both subclavian veins compressed with no flow"*

Six years after the first positive study, bilateral vascular occlusion is documented on dynamic testing.

### 2024: MRA Confirmation

**July 2024 — MRA, Royal Free Hospital**

Mr Aaron Goh documented:
> *"MRA... confirms compression and focal stenosis of subclavian arteries bilaterally"*

Multiple imaging modalities now confirm the same pathology.

## Why The Delay?

### Static vs Dynamic Imaging

TOS is a **positional** condition — compression occurs in certain arm positions. Standard imaging may not capture it:

- **Static imaging** (arms down) may appear normal
- **Dynamic imaging** (arms abducted) reveals compression

The 2024 CT radiologist specifically noted this limitation.

### "Functional" Narrative Interference

The inappropriate psychiatric framing may have contributed to delays:
- Symptoms attributed to "somatoform" disorder
- Reluctance to investigate "psychological" symptoms
- Specialist referrals delayed

### Surgical Complexity

TOS surgery (first rib resection) is complex and carries risks. However:
- The decision to not operate should follow from proper assessment
- Conservative management should be planned, not default
- Ongoing suffering requires ongoing review
